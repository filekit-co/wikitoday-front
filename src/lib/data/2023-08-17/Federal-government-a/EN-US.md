<div class="article-body">
The federal cabinet has passed a law for the partial legalization of cannabis, with the aim of curbing the black market. The reactions from Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania, a state in Germany, have been varied.

 Stefanie Drese, the Health Minister of Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania, supports the plans for legalization, highlighting the benefits of licensed distribution in controlling cannabis cultivation and reducing the illegal drug trade. However, Drese emphasizes the importance of protecting children and young people in all deliberations and calls for increased efforts in addiction prevention.

 The FDP parliamentary group in Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania has shown support for making the state a model region. The bill proposes to remove cannabis from the list of prohibited substances, allowing adults to possess up to 25 grams and grow a maximum of three plants for personal use.

<div class="article-body">
Cannabis clubs would be permitted to cultivate and distribute the drug under strict conditions. However, operators of cannabis clubs in Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania are disappointed with the draft law, citing strict documentation requirements and consumption rules that hinder club structures.

 The police union in Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania has expressed concerns about the feasibility of the law, particularly in determining whether drivers are fit to drive after consuming cannabis. Interior Minister Christian Pegel supports legalization, but also highlights the need to ensure traffic safety.

 The State Coordination Office for Addiction Issues in Mecklenburg-Western Pomerania welcomes the partial legalization but calls for additional efforts in prevention and education. The law is expected to come into force by the end of the year, pending approval from the Bundestag and the Bundesrat.
